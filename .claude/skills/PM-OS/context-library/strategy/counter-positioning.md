# Counter-Positioning Strategy

**Purpose:** Framework for building a business model that incumbents can't copy without hurting themselves. Context for differentiation strategy.

**Source:** Hamilton Helmer's "7 Powers" (one of the 7), emphasized in Aakash Gupta's strategy frameworks

---

## What is Counter-Positioning?

**Definition:** A newcomer adopts a superior business model that the incumbent can't mimic because it would damage their existing business.

**Key insight:** The best defense isn't superior execution—it's a model the competition *won't* copy even if they could.

---

## Classic Examples

### Netflix vs Blockbuster

**Netflix's model:**
- Subscription streaming (unlimited content, no late fees)
- All-you-can-watch for $10/month

**Why Blockbuster couldn't copy:**
- 70% of Blockbuster's profit came from late fees
- Retail stores were sunk costs
- Switching would cannibalize existing revenue

**Result:** Blockbuster went bankrupt

---

### Vanguard vs Active Fund Managers

**Vanguard's model:**
- Low-cost index funds (no stock picking)
- Pass savings to investors

**Why active managers couldn't copy:**
- Their entire value prop was "we pick better stocks"
- Low-cost would admit stock picking doesn't work
- Would destroy 80%+ fee revenue

**Result:** Vanguard became largest fund manager

---

### Dollar Shave Club vs Gillette

**Dollar Shave Club's model:**
- Subscription delivery, no retail markup
- Cheap razors mailed to you

**Why Gillette couldn't easily copy:**
- Dominated retail shelf space (sunk cost)
- Retail partnerships would be threatened
- Low prices would hurt high-margin razor blade sales

**Result:** Unilever bought DSC for $1B

## How to Counter-Position

### Step 1: Identify Incumbent's Profit Pool

**Questions to ask:**
- Where do they make most of their money?
- What parts of their model are highest margin?
- What would hurt them most to give up?

**Example: Microsoft in 2000s**
- Profit pool: Windows + Office licenses
- Vulnerable to: Free, cloud-based alternatives

---

### Step 2: Design a Model That Attacks That Pool

**Your model should:**
- Deliver superior customer value
- Threaten the incumbent's cash cow
- Be difficult for them to copy without pain

**Example: Google Docs**
- Free (vs $500 for Microsoft Office)
- Cloud-based (no install, works anywhere)
- Collaborative by default

**Microsoft's dilemma:** Copy it = cannibalize Office revenue

---

### Step 3: Execute Before They Respond

**Counter-positioning has a time limit:**
- Eventually incumbents overcome their inertia
- First-mover advantage matters
- Build defensibility before they catch up

**Example: Microsoft eventually**
- Launched Office 365 (cloud + subscription)
- But Google/Docs had 5+ year head start
- Microsoft lost education market permanently

---

## When Counter-Positioning Works

**✅ Best conditions:**
1. **Incumbent has sunk costs** (retail stores, legacy infrastructure)
2. **Cultural resistance** (sales team compensated on old model)
3. **Brand damage** (copying would admit they were wrong)
4. **Regulatory capture** (they benefit from current regulations)
5. **Organizational complexity** (too big to pivot quickly)

**❌ Won't work when:**
- Incumbent is highly adaptive (Amazon, Apple)
- Your model has no cost advantage
- Switching costs are low for incumbent
- They can launch a separate brand (P&G strategy)

## Modern Counter-Positioning Examples

### Notion vs Microsoft Word/Confluence

**Notion's model:**
- All-in-one workspace (docs + wiki + db)
- Freemium, bottoms-up adoption
- No sales team

**Why incumbents struggled:**
- Enterprise sales teams can't sell $0 product
- Cannibalize multiple product lines
- Freemium threatens pricing power

---

### Figma vs Adobe

**Figma's model:**
- Browser-based (no install)
- Real-time collaboration
- Free for individuals

**Why Adobe resisted (until acquisition):**
- Desktop app legacy (Creative Cloud)
- Enterprise licensing model
- "Professional" brand positioning

## How to Use This Framework

### In Strategy Planning

**Ask yourself:**
1. What business model can we adopt that competitors can't copy?
2. What would competitors have to sacrifice to match us?
3. Do we have 3-5 years before they overcome inertia?

### In PRDs

**Frame your feature as counter-positioning:**
```
**Strategic Rationale:**
This feature strengthens our counter-positioning vs [Incumbent].

By [doing X], we force them to choose between:
- Option A: Ignore us (we win market share)
- Option B: Copy us (cannibalizes their [profit pool])

We expect 2-3 years before they respond, giving us time to build [defensibility].
```

### In Competitive Analysis

**Map the incumbent's vulnerabilities:**
1. Their profit pools (where they make money)
2. Their sunk costs (what they can't abandon)
3. Their cultural inertia (sales comp, org structure)

**Find the gap:** Where can you position that they can't follow?

---

## Counter-Positioning ≠ Just Being Cheap

**Common mistake:** "We'll just charge less"

**Why that's not counter-positioning:**
- They can match your price (price wars)
- Unless lower price comes from structural model difference

**Real counter-positioning:**
- Your model delivers value differently
- Lower cost is a *result* of model, not the strategy itself

**Example:**
- ❌ "We're Uber but cheaper" (they can match price)
- ✅ "We're shared ride-pooling" (different model, sustainable cost advantage)

---

## Combine with Other Powers

**Counter-positioning alone isn't enough:**
- Incumbents will eventually adapt
- You need to build additional defensibility

**Combine with:**
- **Network effects:** Users/data lock-in
- **Switching costs:** Integration depth
- **Scale economies:** Unit economics improve
- **Branding:** Associate with the new model

**Example: Netflix**
1. Started: Counter-positioning (streaming vs rentals)
2. Built: Content library, viewing data
3. Now has: Original content, personalization algorithms
